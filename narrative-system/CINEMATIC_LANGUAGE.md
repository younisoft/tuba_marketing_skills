# Cinematic Language

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md)
> **If Tuba were a film director, this is the director.** Not a genre, not a single filmmaker to imitate — a set of instincts. Where [`VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md`](VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md) covers how a single frame is composed, this covers how frames move, cut, and accumulate into a scene.

---

## Lens

A natural, slightly long lens — close to how the human eye actually perceives distance and compression. No wide-angle distortion exaggerating a room's scale (the real-estate-photography instinct this world explicitly rejects, `WORLD_BUILDING.md`), no extreme telephoto compression that flattens a scene into abstraction. The camera sees the way a person standing in the room would see.

## Movement

Handheld-adjacent, but steady — movement with a human body's subtle imperfection, never a locked-off, sterile static shot, and never a showy crane or drone move that draws attention to the camera itself. When the camera moves, it moves the way a person would move through a space they already know well: unhurried, purposeful, slightly imperfect.

## Editing

Cuts happen on breath, not on beat. This world's editing rhythm follows a person's natural attention — lingering exactly as long as a real moment would hold someone's gaze, then moving on, rather than following an external musical or algorithmic tempo. A cut in this world should feel like blinking, not like a transition being announced.

## Silence

Silence is not empty — it is one of this world's primary tools. A story is allowed to hold a quiet moment without music or dialogue filling it, trusting the image and the moment itself to carry weight. The instinct to fill every second with sound is a marketing reflex this world resists; real life includes silence, and so should this world's stories.

## Pacing

Unhurried, structurally — even a fast-cut, high-energy TikTok execution (`../context-packs/video-production.md`) should feel like it's moving quickly toward something calm, not staying frantic throughout. Pacing in this world always resolves toward stillness; it never escalates toward climax the way a thriller or action trailer would.

## Music

When used, warm and understated — real instrumentation over synthetic, restraint over swelling crescendo. Music in this world supports a moment; it never manufactures an emotion the image and story haven't already earned. Silence is frequently the more powerful choice, and should be considered first, not defaulted away from.

## Transitions

Soft — cross-dissolves, match-cuts on a physical gesture (a hand on a door becoming a hand on a different door, years later), never a hard graphic wipe or a flashy digital transition effect. This mirrors, at the narrative level, the soft ease-in-out motion principle already established at the technical level (`../advertising-system/visual-system.md` §8) — the same instinct, now applied to how scenes connect rather than how a UI element animates.

## Narrative Rhythm

Anthology over epic. This world rarely tells one long, continuous story — it tells many short, specific ones, cut together or released separately, each trusted to stand on its own (`STORY_ARCHITECTURE.md`'s compression rules). The narrative rhythm of the *brand as a whole*, over years, should feel like a well-edited collection of short stories, not a single unfolding saga.

## What Never Appears

- A hard, dramatic score sting timed to a "reveal" of a property
- Rapid, aggressive cutting used to manufacture urgency or excitement that the story hasn't earned
- A voice-of-God narrator explaining what the audience should feel
- Slow-motion used purely for spectacle (a door opening in exaggerated slow-motion, a property "unveiled") — slow motion, when used at all, is reserved for a single, genuinely significant physical gesture (a key turning), never for scale-flattery
- Any transition, cut, or camera move that draws more attention to itself than to the person on screen

---

## The Directorial Test

Before any shot list is built, ask: *would this choice make sense in a quiet, honest short film about someone's real life — or does it only make sense in an advertisement?* If the answer is the latter, the choice belongs somewhere else, not in this world.
