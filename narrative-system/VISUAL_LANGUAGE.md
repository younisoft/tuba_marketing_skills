# Visual Language

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md)
> **Not layouts. Not UI. Perception.** [`../advertising-system/visual-system.md`](../advertising-system/visual-system.md) specifies *how Tuba is built* — hex values, spacing scale, type sizes, component radii. This document specifies *how Tuba sees* — the way a director of photography thinks before a single frame is composed, prior to any technical decision. Nothing here contradicts visual-system.md; everything here explains the perceptual instinct that document's rules were built to protect.

---

## Composition

Tuba's world is composed around **one subject, given room.** Never a crowded frame trying to say three things at once — a single person, a single detail, a single gesture, surrounded by enough negative space that the eye has nowhere else to go. This is not minimalism as an aesthetic trend; it is a direct visual expression of `../advertising-dna.md` Principle 4 ("one idea per asset") applied to composition itself.

## Scale

People are never diminished by their environment in this world. A common real-estate-photography instinct is to make architecture look monumental and people look small inside it — Tuba's visual instinct is the reverse: architecture exists in service of the person inside it, and scale should always make the *human* feel like the largest thing in the frame, emotionally if not literally.

## Space

Negative space is not empty space — it is *breathing room*, used deliberately the way a pause is used in speech. A frame that is too full reads as anxious; this world is never anxious. Space in a Tuba image often does real narrative work: the empty half of a frame is where a thought completes itself, or where a second person is about to enter.

## Perspective

Eye-level, overwhelmingly. This world is witnessed, not surveyed — it avoids the god's-eye drone shot and the dramatic low-angle "hero" shot alike, both of which put the camera (and by extension, the brand) above the subject. The camera in this world sits at the same height as the people in it, because Tuba is a companion inside the story, never a narrator above it (`../advertising-dna.md` Principle 1).

## Rhythm

Visual rhythm here is unhurried and slightly irregular — the rhythm of a real day, not a shot list. A sequence of images should feel like moments a person actually paused to notice, not a systematically art-directed grid. Consistency comes from tone and color discipline (`visual-system.md` §1.7), not from mechanical repetition of framing.

## Texture

Texture is where truth lives in this world — the grain of real wood, the specific wear on a worn doorstep, the slight imperfection in hand-laid stone. A story that looks too smooth, too rendered, too "finished," has drifted out of this world and into generic real-estate marketing (`WORLD_BUILDING.md`'s rejected clichés). Texture is the visual proof of the specificity this entire narrative system depends on.

## Materials

Real, regionally specific materials — Najdi stone, Hejazi coral-stone and wood lattice (rowshan), desert clay, palm-frond shade structures — used accurately, never as generic "Middle Eastern" set-dressing (`CULTURAL_TRUTHS.md`'s discipline applied visually). Where a story is set matters visually, not just narratively.

## Architecture

Architecture in this world is always a *setting*, never the subject (`WORLD_BUILDING.md`'s hard rule that the property is never the protagonist). It should feel inhabited before it's admired — a beautiful, empty architectural hero shot is the wrong instinct here; a slightly imperfect, clearly lived-in space is the right one.

## Environment

Saudi geography is a character with real range — Najd's flatness and big open sky, the Hejaz coastline's water light, the Eastern Province's palm groves, Asir's green mountain air. The environment should always be specific to where a story is actually set, never a placeholder "desert" backdrop standing in for the whole country (`CULTURAL_TRUTHS.md`).

## Light

Warm, motivated, naturalistic — light that appears to come from a real, identifiable source (a window, a doorway, the sun at a specific hour), never flat studio lighting with no apparent origin. Golden hour is the default register (`visual-system.md` §6.1), but this world also trusts harsher midday light and the specific blue of early evening when a story calls for them — light should match the emotional hour of the story, not just look pleasant.

## Shadow

Shadow is not the absence of light in this world — it is *relief made visible* (`SYMBOL_LIBRARY.md`'s Shade entry). A deep, cool shadow cast by a wall, a tree, or a covered walkway is one of this world's most emotionally loaded images, precisely because it is never explained. It should simply feel like relief, the way stepping into real shade does.

---

## The Perceptual Test

Before any frame is composed, ask: *does this look like something a person paused to actually notice, or does it look like something built to be photographed?* Everything in this document exists to keep the answer on the right side of that line.
