# World Building

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md) · **Builds on:** [`NARRATIVE_MANIFESTO.md`](NARRATIVE_MANIFESTO.md)
> **Purpose:** every story world has boundaries — things that are true inside it and things that simply don't happen there, the way it never rains indoors in a Wes Anderson film or nothing is ever truly ugly in a Pixar one. This document draws Tuba's boundaries so every future story, regardless of who tells it, is recognizably set in the same world.

---

## What Exists Inside This World

- **Specific people, never types.** Everyone who appears has a particular reason, a particular history, a particular detail that couldn't belong to anyone else. No generic "family" or "young couple" — always someone in particular.
- **Unfinished lives.** Nobody in this world has already arrived. Every story is mid-chapter — someone building toward something, not someone who has already achieved a static, completed ideal.
- **Real weather, real light, real time of day.** The world runs on golden hour, midday heat, the specific blue of a Gulf evening, the sound of Maghrib approaching — not a studio's idea of "nice light."
- **Quiet competence.** Guidance happens here, often, but it is never showy. A guide in this world solves things before they become visible problems, and rarely announces having done so.
- **Rooms that have been lived in.** A cup left on a counter. A shoe by the door. A wall with a height chart in pencil. Texture that proves a life is actually happening, not staged for a photograph.
- **Thresholds.** Doors, gates, front steps — the physical moment of crossing from outside to inside recurs constantly, because it is the world's central ritual (see [`SYMBOL_LIBRARY.md`](SYMBOL_LIBRARY.md)).
- **Distance and return.** People leave this world and come back to it — for work, for study, for pilgrimage, for war, for simply growing up and moving out. The world always has room for someone walking back toward it.
- **Saudi Arabia, specifically and confidently** — not an abstracted "Middle East," not a stand-in for anywhere. Najd's flatness, Hejaz's coast, the Eastern Province's palm groves, Asir's mountains — each a genuinely different physical world, all inside this one.

## What Never Exists Inside This World

- **No aspirational strangers.** Nobody in this world is styled to look like a stock-photography version of success — no staged laughter at nothing, no cast of models standing at unnatural distances from each other.
- **No perfect houses with no one in them.** An empty, pristine, architecturally "hero" shot with no human trace is a real-estate-brochure image, not a Tuba story. If a space appears with no one in it, it must still show evidence someone was just there.
- **No glass towers as the default image of ambition.** They exist in Saudi Arabia and can appear when a story is genuinely about one — but they are never used as shorthand for "success" or "the future," because that shorthand belongs to every competitor's category cliché (see `BIG_IDEA_PLATFORM.md` Part One, cliché #7).
- **No countdown clocks, no "act now," no manufactured scarcity.** This world does not manufacture urgency — see [`EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md`](EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md) and the hard boundaries already governing the whole platform in [`../CLAUDE_SYSTEM_PROMPT.md`](../CLAUDE_SYSTEM_PROMPT.md) §7, which this world inherits without exception.
- **No brand-as-hero.** Tuba never appears in this world as the protagonist of its own story — only as the presence that made someone else's possible (`advertising-dna.md` Principle 1, carried forward here as a world law, not just a copy rule).
- **No literal depiction of paradise or religious iconography**, even though the brand's own name carries that resonance (`BIG_IDEA_PLATFORM.md` Part Two). The world can imply shade, shelter, and blessing structurally — through light, trees, threshold, rest — but never illustrates Jannah directly. This is a hard boundary, not a stylistic preference.
- **No status displays.** No one in this world is shown to prove they've "made it" to anyone else. Pride here is private, not performed (see [`EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md`](EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md) on Pride).
- **No condescension toward beginners.** A first-time buyer's confusion is never played for humor or contrast against someone more "sophisticated."

## What Emotions Dominate

Belonging, first. Quiet relief, second. A very specific, private kind of pride, third — never loud, never performed for an audience inside the story itself. Full mapping, including when each is *not* appropriate: [`EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md`](EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md).

## What Visual Language Dominates

Warm, natural light; real materials (stone, wood grain, worn tile); rounded architectural lines echoing the brand mark's own geometry (`current-brand-analysis.md` §2); negative space that gives a moment room to breathe rather than filling every frame. Full language: [`VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md`](VISUAL_LANGUAGE.md), [`PHOTOGRAPHY_LANGUAGE.md`](PHOTOGRAPHY_LANGUAGE.md).

## What Kinds of Stories Naturally Belong

- A story where the "home" turns out to be something nobody would have put on a brochure — a modest apartment with one perfect window, a shared family house being reconfigured for a new generation, a first rental that's small but genuinely, finally theirs.
- A story about the gap between what someone expected to want and what they actually needed — the plot twist is always personal, never architectural.
- A story where the guide's role is nearly invisible — felt in the ease of the outcome, rarely staged as a dramatic rescue.
- A story that ends on one unrepeatable detail, not a general statement of happiness (see [`STORY_ARCHITECTURE.md`](STORY_ARCHITECTURE.md)'s Arrival beat).
- A story about someone returning — to a city, a neighborhood, a family house — after years away.
- A quiet, low-event story: nothing "happens" except someone finally exhales. This world trusts stillness as much as incident.

## What Kinds of Stories Never Belong

- A story that resolves through a discount, an offer, or a deadline. The resolution in this world is always emotional or relational, never transactional (transactional resolution belongs in `advertising-system/campaign-playbook.md`'s performance-marketing register, not here).
- A story where the property is the main character. The property is always a setting for someone else's story, never the subject of its own.
- A story that uses fear as its engine — a scary landlord, a home-buying horror story used for shock value. This world can *acknowledge* real anxiety (see `HUMAN_TRUTHS.md`), but it never dramatizes it for tension the way a thriller would.
- A story that flattens Saudi life into a single aesthetic (only ultra-modern, or only ultra-traditional). The world contains real range — see [`CULTURAL_TRUTHS.md`](CULTURAL_TRUTHS.md).
- A story about winning, beating, or outcompeting another buyer. Competition between people over a place has no home in this world — only a person finding *their* place, never taking it from someone else's.
- Comedy at the expense of a character's inexperience, class, region, or dialect.

## The One-Sentence World Rule

If a story could be set in *any* real estate brand's universe without changing a single detail, it does not belong in this one. Every story here must contain at least one detail that only makes sense because it's specifically, personally *theirs* — the same discipline the BIG IDEA itself depends on (`BIG_IDEA_PLATFORM.md` Part Eight's stress-test finding on specificity).
