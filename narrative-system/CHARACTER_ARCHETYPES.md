# Character Archetypes

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md)
> **Not personas.** [`../brand-intelligence/buyer-personas.md`](../brand-intelligence/buyer-personas.md), `seller-personas.md`, `broker-personas.md`, `developer-personas.md`, and `investor-personas.md` define *marketing segments* — goals, objections, CTAs, content preferences, used to target and brief real campaigns. The nine archetypes below are **story roles** — recurring human shapes a narrative can be built around, the way a filmmaker thinks about a character's function in a story rather than their demographic profile. One marketing persona can express itself through several different archetypes depending on which story is being told about them, and vice versa.

Each archetype recurs across campaigns the way a company of actors recurs across a director's films — not the same literal person each time, but a familiar human shape audiences learn to recognize.

---

## 1. The Dream Builder

**Who they are:** someone building toward a specific future they can already see clearly, even though it doesn't exist yet — a person mid-plan, not mid-panic.
**What they want:** to make a private vision real, on their own terms and timeline.
**What they fear:** compromising the vision to make it easier, and losing the thing that made it theirs in the first place.
**Recurring role:** the protagonist of pillar *Your Particular Reasons* and *Building What Outlasts You* — often, though not exclusively, a developer, an investor, or a family planning years ahead.
**Representative beat:** sketching a room's layout on a napkin years before it exists, and recognizing it, later, exactly as drawn.

## 2. The First Home Seeker

**Who they are:** someone crossing a threshold they've never crossed before — not necessarily young, just new to this specific decision.
**What they want:** proof that they haven't missed something a more experienced person would have caught.
**What they fear:** being taken advantage of, or making an irreversible mistake out of inexperience.
**Recurring role:** the protagonist of pillar *First Times* — the archetype most often paired with The Guide.
**Representative beat:** the moment fear turns into readiness — not a dramatic reveal, just a quiet internal shift from "I don't think I can" to "I think I can."

## 3. The Family Protector

**Who they are:** someone making a housing decision on behalf of people they love, not primarily for themselves.
**What they want:** a decision they can defend to the people they're protecting, years from now, without regret.
**What they fear:** choosing wrong and having someone else absorb the consequences.
**Recurring role:** protagonist of *What Gets Passed Down*, frequent supporting presence in *First Times* and *Still Becoming*.
**Representative beat:** noticing a detail no one else in the family would think to check — a school's walking distance, a wall that could hold a swing — and quietly making sure it's accounted for.

## 4. The Patient Investor

**Who they are:** someone who thinks in years and decades, not weeks — deliberately unhurried, allergic to hype.
**What they want:** to be right for reasons they can explain, not reasons they were persuaded of.
**What they fear:** being sold a story instead of shown a number.
**Recurring role:** protagonist of *Building What Outlasts You* — the one archetype whose stories should lead with data before feeling (mirrors `copywriting.md` §5.7's investment-copy register, at the narrative level).
**Representative beat:** the calm, almost anticlimactic moment of confirming a decision was sound — no fireworks, just quiet vindication.

## 5. The Returning Son *(or daughter — the archetype is not gendered; named for its most common cultural framing)*

**Who they are:** someone who left — for study, for work, for any reason — and is coming back, changed, to a place that hasn't entirely changed with them.
**What they want:** to belong again to a place they've grown apart from and grown to miss in equal measure.
**What they fear:** finding that the place they're returning to no longer has room for who they've become.
**Recurring role:** protagonist of *The Long Way Back*.
**Representative beat:** noticing something achingly unchanged — a specific smell, a specific corner shop — and feeling, for a second, like no time passed at all.

## 6. The Quiet Achiever

**Who they are:** someone who has earned something significant and has zero interest in announcing it.
**What they want:** a place that reflects their standards without performing them for anyone else.
**What they fear:** being marketed to as if status were the point.
**Recurring role:** protagonist of *Your Particular Reasons* and *The Unphotographed Rooms* — the primary archetype for luxury-register storytelling, executed through restraint per [`../brand-intelligence/luxury-framework.md`](../brand-intelligence/luxury-framework.md), never through display.
**Representative beat:** choosing the plain door over the grand one, because the plain one is the one that's actually theirs.

## 7. The Optimist

**Who they are:** someone early in life or in a decision, moving toward it with more hope than caution — not naïve, just not yet worn down by the process.
**What they want:** for the hope to survive contact with the actual, complicated process.
**What they fear:** cynicism creeping in before they even get there.
**Recurring role:** frequent secondary presence across nearly every pillar — the character whose optimism the story either protects or (rarely, carefully) tests.
**Representative beat:** saying something sincere and slightly too hopeful out loud, and having it turn out to be true anyway.

## 8. The Guide

**Who they are:** the agent, broker, or advisor — present in most stories, central character in almost none.
**What they want:** for the story to end well for someone else, without needing credit for it.
**What they fear:** being seen as a salesperson instead of what they actually are.
**Recurring role:** the human embodiment of the Sage archetype already defined at the brand-voice level (`advertising-dna.md` §6) — protagonist only in pillar *The People Who Carried You There*, supporting presence everywhere else.
**Representative beat:** the detail nobody else notices — catching a problem before it becomes one, then saying almost nothing about having done so.

## 9. The Neighbor

**Who they are:** not a customer at all — the person already there, who welcomes, warns, or simply witnesses someone else's arrival.
**What they want:** the street, building, or community to keep being a good one.
**What they fear:** change that erodes what made the place worth belonging to in the first place.
**Recurring role:** grounding presence in *The Long Way Back* and *Still Becoming* — the character who proves a place was real and lived-in before the story's protagonist arrived.
**Representative beat:** the first knock on the door after moving in — someone from three houses down, arriving with nothing but a plate of food and no ulterior motive.

---

## How Archetypes Are Used (and Misused)

**Correct use:** an archetype is a narrative *function* a real, specific person fulfills in a given story — the story is always about one particular First Home Seeker, never "a First Home Seeker" in the abstract (see `WORLD_BUILDING.md`'s rule against generic "types").

**Misuse to avoid:** treating an archetype as a costume to put on a stock model, or assuming one marketing persona maps to exactly one archetype forever. A Luxury Buyer persona (`brand-intelligence/buyer-personas.md` §2) might appear as The Quiet Achiever in one story and The Family Protector in another — the archetype is chosen by what the *story* needs, not by which segment the person belongs to.
