# Motion Language

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md)
> **Not easing curves.** [`../advertising-system/visual-system.md`](../advertising-system/visual-system.md) §8 already specifies the technical motion system — cubic-bezier values, transition durations, the rule that the logo mark may only scale/fade. This document explains the *philosophy* those specs were written to protect: not how fast something moves, but why it should move that way at all.

---

## How Tuba Moves

Slowly enough to be trusted. Nothing in this world moves faster than a person could naturally move through it — no whip-pans, no speed-ramped reveals, no motion graphics racing to fit information into a short attention span. If a story needs to move quickly (a fast-cut TikTok edit, `../context-packs/video-production.md`), the *cuts* carry the pace — individual movements within a shot stay unhurried regardless of overall tempo.

## Speed as Meaning, Not Habit

Speed in this world is never a default setting — it's a choice that means something specific each time it's made. A slow push toward a threshold means anticipation. A held, static frame during a Reflection beat (`STORY_ARCHITECTURE.md`) means the story trusts the moment enough not to rush past it. Fast cutting, used sparingly, signals urgency of *life* (a busy morning, a full house) — never urgency of *sale*.

## Transitions

Soft dissolves and match-cuts on physical continuity — a hand, a doorway, a specific gesture that carries from one scene into the next. Hard cuts are reserved for genuine narrative breaks (a jump forward in time), never used as a stylistic flourish. This is the same instinct already governing UI-level motion (`visual-system.md` §8's cross-dissolve/soft-slide rule) — one philosophy, expressed at every scale from a button state to a film cut.

## Animation Principles

Where motion graphics or animated icons appear (`../advertising-system/iconography.md` §1.2's draw-on and scale/fade rules), the underlying principle is growth, not mechanism. An icon drawing itself on should feel like something taking root, not like a loading bar completing. This connects directly to the tree/growth symbolism already established (`SYMBOL_LIBRARY.md`) — motion in this world is one more place that mythology quietly lives, structurally, without ever being explained on screen.

## Pacing

Every piece of Tuba motion content, regardless of length, should have somewhere calm to land. A 15-second Reel that ends mid-energy hasn't finished its story — even the fastest-paced content in this world resolves, in its final beat, toward the Peace or Relief register (`EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md`), the same way `STORY_ARCHITECTURE.md`'s eight-beat structure always moves toward Arrival and Reflection eventually, no matter how compressed the format.

## What This World's Motion Never Does

- Spin, bounce, or distort the brand mark (absolute rule, inherited without exception from `current-brand-analysis.md` §6 and `visual-system.md` §8)
- Use countdown-timer animation or urgency-signaling motion graphics (flashing, pulsing "act now" treatments)
- Speed-ramp toward a property reveal the way a luxury car advertisement might
- Use jarring, high-frequency cuts to simulate energy the story hasn't actually earned
- Move the camera or a graphic element faster than a viewer could comfortably track — this world never asks for more attention than it's earned

---

## The Motion Test

Before animating anything, ask: *if this moved at half the speed, would it still work?* If the answer is no — if the motion is only impressive because it's fast — it's manufacturing energy the story hasn't earned, and it doesn't belong in this world.
