# Human Truths

> **Part of:** [`../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md`](../NARRATIVE_SYSTEM.md)
> **Not about real estate. About life.** One hundred observations about people — the emotional bedrock every future Tuba story can draw a single line from. None of these mention Tuba, a platform, a listing, or a transaction, on purpose. If a truth needs the word "home" to make sense, it's doing too little work.
> **How to use this document:** a story doesn't need to *state* one of these truths. It needs to be built quietly on top of one, the way a building is built on a foundation nobody sees once it's finished.

---

## I. Memory & Time

1. People don't remember square meters. They remember the light at 4pm.
2. Nobody describes their childhood home by its address. They describe it by a smell.
3. A house becomes a home the first time something in it breaks and gets fixed by someone who loves you.
4. The first night in a new place is never about the bed. It's about learning where the light switches are in the dark.
5. People remember the sound a door made long after they've forgotten what the door looked like.
6. Nobody remembers signing the paperwork. Everyone remembers the walk to the car afterward.
7. Time doesn't make a place a home. Repetition does — the same walk, the same chair, a thousand ordinary times.
8. People measure a decade of their life not in years but in which house they were living in when it happened.
9. A place becomes irreplaceable the moment it holds a memory that couldn't have happened anywhere else.
10. Most people can't describe their dream home. They can describe the one afternoon that made them want it.

## II. Identity & Belonging

11. People don't move to escape a place. They move to become someone the old place couldn't hold.
12. Belonging isn't about being from somewhere. It's about not having to explain yourself once you're there.
13. Everyone is fluent in at least one room where they don't have to perform.
14. A person's real address is wherever they stop bracing for judgment.
15. Nobody wants to be understood in general. They want to be understood specifically.
16. People don't want a home that impresses visitors. They want one that makes them exhale once the door closes behind the last guest.
17. The self a person is at home is usually the truest version, and the one they're least practiced at showing anyone else.
18. Everyone secretly believes their own definition of comfort is slightly unreasonable. It rarely is.
19. A person's taste in homes says more about their childhood than their income.
20. Nobody wants to be the exception to a rule. They want the rule to have never applied to them in the first place.

## III. Family & Generations

21. Every family has one room that quietly runs the household, and it's rarely the one on the floor plan labeled "living."
22. Children don't remember the size of the house. They remember whether someone was home.
23. Parents don't want the biggest house for their children. They want the one their children will want to come back to.
24. Every generation thinks it's the first to worry about the next one. None of them are.
25. A grandmother rearranging the furniture isn't being difficult. She's teaching the house how to hold a family again.
26. Siblings remember the same house completely differently, and both of them are right.
27. The house someone grew up resenting is often the one they most want to recreate, quietly, for their own children.
28. Nobody plans to become the parent who worries about school proximity. Everyone does.
29. A family doesn't outgrow a house. It outgrows the version of itself the house was built for.
30. The people who visit rarely remember the furniture. They remember whether they were fed.

## IV. Fear & Trust

31. Most fear isn't of making the wrong decision. It's of finding out later, from someone else, that they did.
32. People don't fear big mistakes. They fear the small detail nobody warned them about.
33. Trust isn't given to whoever asks for it first. It's given to whoever explains without being asked.
34. Nobody trusts a promise. Everyone trusts a person who admits what they don't know.
35. The fastest way to lose someone's trust is to answer the question they didn't ask instead of the one they did.
36. People forgive a slow answer. They rarely forgive a confident wrong one.
37. Most people aren't afraid of paperwork. They're afraid of not understanding it, and too embarrassed to say so.
38. Everyone has, at some point, pretended to understand something to avoid looking foolish. Almost everyone regrets it.
39. A person's guard drops the moment they realize no one is trying to sell them something.
40. Nobody wants to be protected from bad news. They want to be told it by someone who stays on the line afterward.

## V. Dreams & Ambition

41. A dream without a specific detail in it is just a wish, and wishes rarely move anyone anywhere.
42. Most people don't dream in floor plans. They dream in a single, specific Tuesday morning.
43. Ambition is quieter than it looks. Most of it happens at a kitchen table after everyone else is asleep.
44. People don't want more than they need. They want proof that what they need is finally, actually possible.
45. The size of a dream has nothing to do with the size of the property it eventually lives in.
46. Everyone imagines success as a single moment. It's almost always a slow accumulation nobody photographs.
47. A person's real ambition is rarely the one they say out loud first.
48. People chase stability their whole lives and call it, once they finally have it, freedom.
49. Nobody wants to be told their dream is realistic. They want to be shown it's already closer than they thought.
50. The bravest financial decision most people ever make is deciding they're allowed to want something at all.

## VI. Change & Transition

51. People don't fear change. They fear changing without anyone noticing.
52. A move is never really about distance. Even ten minutes away can feel like starting over.
53. The hardest part of any transition isn't the move itself — it's the week before, when the old life and the new one both feel unreal.
54. Nobody grieves a house. They grieve who they were the last time they were happy in it.
55. Growth rarely announces itself. It's usually noticed only in hindsight, when someone realizes they can't remember not knowing something.
56. People don't resist change. They resist being changed by something they didn't choose.
57. The bravest part of starting over is usually the most boring part — the first grocery run in an unfamiliar aisle.
58. Endings are rarely as dramatic as beginnings. Most people leave a place the same ordinary way they arrived at it.
59. A person adjusts to a new home the way they adjust to grief — slower than expected, and then all at once.
60. Nothing ages a person faster than realizing the home of their childhood no longer needs them in it.

## VII. Everyday Life & Ritual

61. A morning routine is the truest autobiography most people will ever write, and they never think to call it one.
62. People don't need a big kitchen. They need the exact corner where the coffee gets made.
63. Ritual isn't about repetition. It's about proving, every day, that a place still recognizes you.
64. The most honest room in any home is the one nobody cleans before guests arrive.
65. Silence is a luxury most people don't realize they're paying for until they finally have it.
66. Everyone has a specific chair. Nobody can explain why it's that one.
67. The smell of a home changes before anyone notices, and by the time they do, it already feels like theirs.
68. People don't decorate to impress visitors. They decorate to remind themselves who they are when no one's watching.
69. A house becomes familiar the day someone stops needing the light on to find their way at night.
70. The ordinary Tuesday is what most people are actually homesick for, not the holidays.

## VIII. Money & Value

71. People don't calculate value in currency. They calculate it in relief.
72. Nobody wants the cheapest option. They want to stop feeling watched for how much they can afford.
73. A fair price feels less like a number and more like being taken seriously.
74. People forgive an honest "this costs more than you hoped" faster than a dishonest "this is a great deal."
75. Most people don't want to negotiate. They want to not have to.
76. Financial anxiety rarely shows up as a number. It shows up as sleeplessness at 2am with no obvious cause.
77. Nobody regrets spending on the room they actually use. Everyone regrets spending on the one meant to impress.
78. A monthly number someone can say out loud without flinching is worth more to them than a lower total they can't.
79. People trust a person who tells them "you don't need that" more than one who's never said it.
80. The real cost of a decision is measured less in money and more in how many nights of sleep it costs.

## IX. Community & Others

81. A neighborhood isn't a location. It's the number of people who'd notice if something was wrong.
82. Hospitality isn't an event. It's what a household does automatically, before it has time to think about it.
83. People don't want to be the most interesting person on their street. They want to be the one their street would help first.
84. Community is built less by grand gestures and more by remembering small details about each other.
85. A guest can tell within a minute whether hospitality is a habit or a performance.
86. Most people learn a place is home the day a stranger's kindness in it stops surprising them.
87. Nobody wants perfect neighbors. They want honest ones.
88. The measure of a good street isn't its appearance. It's whether someone would knock if the lights stayed off too long.
89. People remember who showed up during a hard week far longer than who showed up to a celebration.
90. A sense of community rarely announces itself. It's noticed only in its absence.

## X. Hope & Meaning

91. Hope isn't the belief that everything will work out. It's the willingness to keep planning as if it might.
92. Every person is, quietly, the hero of a story only they can see the whole shape of.
93. Meaning rarely arrives in the big moment. It arrives in the very next ordinary one, the first time it's noticed.
94. People don't want their life to look impressive. They want it to feel, privately, like it was worth the trouble.
95. The proudest moment in most people's lives was never photographed.
96. Everyone is trying to build a life their younger self would recognize as the one they were hoping for.
97. Contentment isn't the absence of wanting more. It's wanting exactly what's already there.
98. A good life isn't the one with the fewest problems. It's the one with people willing to help solve them.
99. Nobody wants to be told they've arrived. They want to feel, quietly, that they have.
100. In the end, almost no one measures their life by what they owned. They measure it by where they were finally, fully themselves.

---

## How These Truths Are Meant to Be Used

Pick one. Build a single story on top of it. Never cite it, never quote it in the finished work — the moment a truth is stated out loud in a piece of creative, it stops being felt and starts being explained, and `WORLD_BUILDING.md` already rules out anything that announces its own meaning instead of earning it.
