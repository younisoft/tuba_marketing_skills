# Creative Principles — Tuba

> **Layer:** Brand Intelligence (static)
> **Purpose:** the small set of durable creative heuristics that sit above any single format or channel rule — the judgment calls a designer or copywriter should be able to make instinctively once internalized, the way Dieter Rams' ten principles or IKEA's Content DNA (content refrences/ikea_content_strategy_report.html, referenced throughout copywriting.md) function for their respective organizations.

## The Ten Principles

### 1. The customer is always the hero.
Tuba is the guide, never the subject of the sentence when the customer's life could be instead. If a piece of creative can be rewritten with the customer as the grammatical subject and it gets stronger, rewrite it (advertising-dna.md Principle 1).

### 2. One idea, said plainly, beats three ideas said cleverly.
Real estate creative is naturally cluttered — price, specs, location, CTA, license. Discipline means picking the one thing this asset needs to say and letting everything else be secondary information (advertising-dna.md Principle 4, copywriting.md §2.2).

### 3. Warmth must be provable.
Every warm claim pairs with a checkable fact. A brand that says "trust us" without a mechanism is indistinguishable from any other brand that says "trust us" (advertising-dna.md §8, prompt-patterns.md §3).

### 4. If it could run for a competitor unchanged, it has failed.
This is the sharpest single test in the system (advertising-dna.md §1). Every headline, visual concept, and campaign idea should be checked against it before it ships.

### 5. Specificity is the whole job.
Abstraction is what generic AI-generated and generic industry advertising both default to. A real number, a real street, a real sensory detail — this is what separates Tuba's creative from the category's (prompt-patterns.md §5, competitor-analysis.md §7).

### 6. Restraint is a luxury signal; noise never is.
This applies beyond the explicit luxury segment — even mass-market Tuba creative should feel considered, not crowded. Negative space, one focal point, one CTA (visual-system.md §1.7, design-review-checklist.md §1).

### 7. Compliance is a design element, not a disclaimer.
The FAL/license badge is built to be elegant and small, not bolted on as legal fine print — because REGA itself designed the regulation to allow this (competitor-analysis.md §11, design-components.md §2.1). Trust and craft are not in tension.

### 8. Bilingual means two original languages, not one translation.
Arabic is conceived at brief stage, not generated from a finished English draft. This is structural to the brand's identity (current-brand-analysis.md §2), not a production step.

### 9. Every emotional claim needs a concrete image behind it.
"You'll feel at home" is a label. "The kind of quiet where you can hear the AC hum from the next room" is a feeling, created rather than named (prompt-patterns.md §5).

### 10. The system exists to be used, not admired.
A rule that's too complicated to apply under real deadline pressure will get skipped. Every principle, checklist, and framework in this system should be usable by a working designer or copywriter mid-project, not just readable in a strategy review.

## How to Apply These Principles

When two rules from elsewhere in the system seem to conflict, or a genuinely new creative situation isn't covered by an existing document, these ten principles are the tiebreaker — apply them in order of the numbering above, and if still unresolved, escalate per [advertising-playbook.md §6](../advertising-system/advertising-playbook.md)'s exception process rather than guessing.

## Best Practices
- New team members (human or AI) should be able to internalize these ten and predict most of the system's other rules correctly from them alone — if a rule elsewhere in the system seems to contradict one of these ten, that's worth flagging for review, not silently following
- Revisit this document at the start of any major rebrand or strategy shift before touching any execution-layer document

## Common Mistakes
- Treating these as inspirational statements rather than literal decision-making tools — each one should be directly applicable to a real creative choice, not just a poster on the wall
- Letting channel/format pressure (a tight deadline, a platform trend) override Principle 4 or Principle 5 — these two are the most commonly tempting to skip under pressure and the most damaging to skip
