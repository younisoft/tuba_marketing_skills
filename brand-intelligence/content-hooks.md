# Content Hooks — Tuba

> **Layer:** Brand Intelligence (static)
> **Purpose:** the definitive library of opening techniques — by format context — for the Hook stage of the Guidance Framework ([copywriting.md §1](../advertising-system/copywriting.md)). Where [headline-library.md](headline-library.md) covers headline formulas by content *category*, this document covers openings by *format/attention context* — the first 3 seconds, first sentence, first paragraph, and platform-specific opening moments.
> **Owner:** Copy + Design leads jointly (hooks are often both visual and verbal)
> **Review frequency:** semi-annually
> **Related documents:** [copywriting.md §1](../advertising-system/copywriting.md), [social-media-playbook.md](../advertising-system/social-media-playbook.md), [prompt-patterns.md §1](../advertising-system/prompt-patterns.md)

## 1. First 3 Seconds (video/motion content)

**Technique:** simultaneous visual + text-overlay hook, no slow build-up.
**Why it works psychologically:** attention on scroll-based platforms is allocated in fractions of a second — a viewer decides whether to keep watching before conscious evaluation kicks in, so the hook must be legible instantly, without sound, without context.
**Application:** social-media-playbook.md §3's TikTok structure (0-2s Hook) is the strict version of this rule.
**Example pattern:** on-screen text fragment ("3 mistakes every buyer makes") appearing the instant a strong visual (a specific property detail, a real person's face) appears — never a logo bumper or slow fade-in before the hook lands.

## 2. First Sentence (written copy — captions, emails, articles)

**Technique:** name the human moment before anything about Tuba (copywriting.md §1 Stage 1).
**Why it works psychologically:** readers scan the first sentence to decide relevance; a sentence about the reader's life earns more continued attention than a sentence about the brand's offering (classic "what's in it for me" filtering).
**Example pattern:** "Ever imagined the street your kids grow up on?" — not "Tuba is excited to announce..."

## 3. First Paragraph (long-form — blog, landing page, email body)

**Technique:** Hook sentence, then one sentence of Recognize (name the specific scenario), before any Solve-stage content appears.
**Why it works psychologically:** long-form content earns continued reading through progressive relevance confirmation — the first paragraph's job is entirely to convince the reader "this is written for someone like me," not to inform yet.
**Example pattern:** "Ever imagined the street your kids grow up on? Maybe it's a quiet cul-de-sac in Riyadh. Maybe it's five minutes from the Corniche in Jeddah." (copywriting.md §1 worked example, Stages 1-2)

## 4. Carousel Opening (Slide 1)

**Technique:** headline-only, no supporting body copy, must work as a stand-alone stop-scroll cover (visual-system.md §9.2).
**Why it works psychologically:** carousel completion rates depend entirely on whether Slide 1 earns the first swipe — it functions like a headline and a thumbnail simultaneously, not like the first paragraph of an article.
**Example pattern:** a single bold statement or question, large type, minimal visual clutter — see the carousel template in ai-prompt-library.md §2.

## 5. Video Opening (long-form — YouTube)

**Technique:** state the viewer benefit directly within the first 5 seconds (social-media-playbook.md §8).
**Why it works psychologically:** long-form video requires a larger attention commitment than short-form — viewers need an explicit reason the investment is worth it, stated plainly, before any scene-setting.
**Example pattern:** "By the end of this video, you'll know exactly how much down payment you actually need." — direct value statement, not a narrative cold open.

## 6. Reel Opening

**Technique:** visual pattern-interrupt (an unexpected first frame) combined with a text-overlay fragment.
**Why it works psychologically:** Reels compete against an infinite, fast-moving feed — a hook needs to visually differ from the preceding content type (e.g., a close-up detail shot instead of another wide property exterior) to interrupt the scroll reflex before the verbal hook even registers.
**Application:** social-media-playbook.md §2's Instagram guidance — "Hook in first 3 seconds of video."

## 7. Story Opening

**Technique:** immediate, single-message clarity — no build-up, because Stories are swiped past even faster than feed content.
**Why it works psychologically:** Stories are consumed in a rapid tap-through rhythm; a viewer's default action is to tap forward unless something stops them in under a second — the opening frame must *be* the message, not introduce it.
**Application:** social-media-playbook.md §4's Snapchat guidance — "the Hook stage should be an image/moment before any text."

## 8. Cross-Format Hook Principles

| Principle | Applies to |
|---|---|
| Specificity beats abstraction (prompt-patterns.md §5) | All formats |
| The reader/viewer is the subject, not Tuba (advertising-dna.md Principle 1) | All formats |
| Visual and verbal hooks should land simultaneously, not sequentially, in video/motion formats | Video, Reels, Stories |
| Written hooks should be a complete thought even out of context (e.g., visible in a notification preview or search snippet) | Email subject lines, push notifications, article previews |

## 9. Hook Failure Patterns to Avoid

| Failure | Why it fails |
|---|---|
| Logo/brand intro before the hook | Wastes the only guaranteed attention window |
| A feature statement as the opening line | Fails the "what's in it for me" filter — see prompt-patterns.md §1 |
| A hook that requires prior context to make sense | Most viewers arrive with zero context — the hook must work cold |
| A generic emotional label instead of a specific image (prompt-patterns.md §5) | Reads as hollow, doesn't actually trigger the intended feeling |

## Best Practices
- Test every hook by removing everything after it — does the hook alone make someone want to know more?
- Match hook intensity to format: written long-form can afford one full sentence of Hook; video/motion has closer to one second

## Common Mistakes
- Applying a written-content hook technique to video (e.g., a slow narrative build that works in an article but loses viewers in the first second of a Reel)
- Treating the hook as a teaser that withholds information rather than a genuine, specific point of relevance
