# Psychological Triggers — Tuba

> **Part of:** [Tuba Advertising Identity System](../ADVERTISING_IDENTITY_GUIDE.md) — Execution Layer
> **Governs against:** [advertising-dna.md §5](advertising-dna.md) (Forbidden Territory) — every trigger below is defined with its guardrail *first*, because Tuba's entire differentiation strategy (competitor-analysis.md §11) depends on not using persuasion the way every other platform in the category does.

**The standard this document holds itself to:** a persuasion technique is only approved if it works by making something *true and relevant* more vivid — never by manufacturing pressure, fear, or a fact that isn't real. If a technique requires inventing urgency, scarcity, or proof, it's not on this list.

---

## 1. Authority

**What it is:** citing credible expertise or regulatory standing to reduce perceived risk.

**WHEN to use:** trust/compliance content, market-insight content, any claim about licensing or verification.

**WHEN NOT to use:** as a substitute for actually being right — authority framing on an unsourced or shaky claim is worse than no claim at all.

**Example:** *"Every listing carries a FAL license number, issued by REGA — verify it yourself in seconds."* (Real authority: the regulator, not Tuba's own say-so.)

**Guardrail:** never claim authority Tuba doesn't have (e.g., implying regulatory status it doesn't hold) — see advertising-dna.md §8 on enforcement matching claims.

---

## 2. Social Proof

**What it is:** showing that real people have made this choice successfully.

**WHEN to use:** community/customer-success content (content-system.md pillar 4), testimonial cards (design-components.md §5.1).

**WHEN NOT to use:** when the proof isn't real. Full stop — no exceptions.

**Example:** a genuine customer quote, attributed accurately, describing a real outcome.

**Guardrail:** **never fabricate reviews, testimonials, user counts, or "X people viewed this today" mechanics** (copywriting.md §9, advertising-dna.md §5). This is the single most tempting trigger to fake and the one most damaging to the Brand Promise if faked and discovered.

---

## 3. Scarcity

**What it is:** signaling limited availability.

**WHEN to use:** **only when genuinely true** — e.g., a specific unit count that is factually limited ("3 units remaining in this phase" where that is literally accurate).

**WHEN NOT to use:** ever, as a manufactured device ("only 2 left!" on unlimited inventory, or repeated indefinitely across "last chance" messaging that never actually runs out).

**Example (approved):** *"This phase has 3 units remaining — the next phase opens in [month]."* (Factual, informative, not pressuring.)

**Guardrail:** this is explicitly named as forbidden territory in advertising-dna.md §5 when fabricated — real scarcity is fine to state plainly; invented scarcity is never fine.

---

## 4. Urgency

**What it is:** motivating faster action via a time constraint.

**WHEN to use:** when a real deadline exists (a school-year relocation window, a genuine financing-offer expiration date, an actual auction/bidding close).

**WHEN NOT to use:** artificial countdown timers, "act now" language with no real deadline behind it, or urgency stacked onto every single piece of content regardless of context.

**Example (approved):** *"Settle in before the first day of school"* (campaign-playbook.md §1.6) — the real external deadline does the work, not manufactured pressure.

**Guardrail:** Tuba's brand promise is relief, not pressure (advertising-dna.md §1) — urgency copy should always be checkable against a real calendar date.

---

## 5. Exclusivity

**What it is:** signaling that something is available to a limited or specific group.

**WHEN to use:** luxury-segment campaigns (campaign-playbook.md §2.6), curated distribution to a specific investor list, genuinely limited partnership programs.

**WHEN NOT to use:** as a default register for mass-market content — Tuba is accessible-premium by default, not exclusive by default (advertising-dna.md §10).

**Example:** curated-distribution luxury property content, sent to a specific list rather than broad targeting — the *mechanism* of exclusivity (who sees it) rather than just the *word* "exclusive" (which is forbidden as a label, copywriting.md §8.3).

**Guardrail:** show exclusivity through actual distribution/access mechanics, never just the adjective.

---

## 6. Trust

**What it is:** reducing perceived risk of the transaction itself.

**WHEN to use:** everywhere, as the connective tissue of the entire Reassure stage (copywriting.md §1) — this is Tuba's most heavily-used trigger by design.

**WHEN NOT to use:** never withhold it, but never state it as a bare adjective either.

**Example:** see [prompt-patterns.md §3](prompt-patterns.md) — every trust claim paired with a specific, checkable mechanism.

**Guardrail:** badges/claims must be operationally true (advertising-dna.md §8) — this is the trigger where the Bayut review-sentiment gap (competitor-analysis.md §4) is the clearest cautionary example in the category.

---

## 7. Belonging

**What it is:** connecting the decision to identity, family, and place, not just utility.

**WHEN to use:** Place & Lifestyle content, National Day/Eid seasonal content, family-audience messaging (messaging-framework.md §8).

**WHEN NOT to use:** B2B/investor content, where data-first register performs better (copywriting.md §5.7).

**Example:** *"The street your kids grow up on"* — belonging expressed through specific imagined detail, not abstract patriotism.

**Guardrail:** keep specific and human — generic "we're all Saudi together" framing reads as hollow compared to a concrete scene.

---

## 8. Security

**What it is:** addressing the fear of an irreversible bad decision.

**WHEN to use:** first-time buyer content, financing/process explainers, any content where the underlying fear is "what if I get this wrong."

**WHEN NOT to use:** don't manufacture a fear that wasn't already present just to resolve it — see Loss Aversion (§11) guardrail below for the related risk.

**Example:** *"Every question you're afraid to ask is one we're used to answering"* (messaging-framework.md §2).

**Guardrail:** name the real fear plainly and respectfully — never exaggerate risk to make Tuba's reassurance feel more necessary than it is.

---

## 9. Investment Confidence

**What it is:** using data to replace anxiety with a sense of informed control.

**WHEN to use:** investor content, market-insight content (copywriting.md §5.7).

**WHEN NOT to use:** consumer-emotional content, where data-first framing feels cold and out of place.

**Example:** *"Average 3-year appreciation in this district: 14%. This is the kind of number that lets you sleep at night."*

**Guardrail:** every number must be real and sourced — this trigger's entire power comes from being more trustworthy than adjective-heavy competitor claims (competitor-analysis.md §9); a single fabricated statistic destroys that advantage permanently.

---

## 10. Future Aspiration

**What it is:** painting a vivid, specific picture of a better future state.

**WHEN to use:** Hook-stage copy (copywriting.md §1), brand/awareness campaigns.

**WHEN NOT to use:** performance/lead-gen content, where specificity about the *present* offer matters more than future imagery (copywriting.md §6).

**Example:** *"Ever imagined a garden your kids grow up in?"*

**Guardrail:** must stay specific and plausible — vague "imagine a better life" aspiration without a concrete image reads as generic AI language (prompt-patterns.md §1, §5).

---

## 11. Loss Aversion

**What it is:** framing around what's avoided/protected, not just what's gained.

**WHEN to use:** sparingly — e.g., "avoid the paperwork mistakes that cost other buyers time and money" framed constructively as a benefit of guidance, not as a scare tactic.

**WHEN NOT to use:** as fear-based urgency ("don't miss out," "everyone else has already found their home") — explicitly forbidden (advertising-dna.md §5, copywriting.md §8.3's comparison-shaming ban).

**Example (approved):** *"Skip the mistakes that cost other buyers weeks — we catch them before they happen."* (Loss framed as something Tuba actively prevents, not something the reader should panic about.)

**Guardrail:** this is the trigger most likely to slide into forbidden territory — always run any loss-aversion copy through the "does this pressure or does this reassure" test before shipping.

---

## 12. Status

**What it is:** connecting a decision to social standing or image.

**WHEN to use:** luxury-segment campaigns only, and even there, subtly — restraint over display (copywriting.md §5.3, advertising-dna.md §5's status-symbol guardrail).

**WHEN NOT to use:** default/mass-market content — Tuba's core promise is relief and belonging, not status (advertising-dna.md §5 explicitly flags status-as-primary-frame as forbidden outside luxury campaigns).

**Example (luxury-only, used carefully):** letting a specific, rare property detail imply status without stating it outright — showing, never labeling (per prompt-patterns.md §2).

**Guardrail:** the most restricted trigger in this system — default to *not* using it unless the campaign brief explicitly calls for luxury-segment register.

## Master Guardrail Table

| Trigger | Default use | Requires real fact behind it? | Approved outside luxury? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authority | Frequent | Yes | Yes |
| Social proof | Frequent | Yes — always | Yes |
| Scarcity | Rare, only if true | Yes — always | Yes, if true |
| Urgency | Rare, only if a real deadline exists | Yes — always | Yes, if true |
| Exclusivity | Luxury-primary | Mechanism-based | Limited |
| Trust | Constant | Yes — always | Yes |
| Belonging | Frequent | N/A (emotional, not factual) | Yes |
| Security | Frequent | N/A | Yes |
| Investment confidence | Frequent (investor content) | Yes — always | Yes |
| Future aspiration | Frequent (Hook stage) | Plausibility required | Yes |
| Loss aversion | Sparing, reframed constructively | N/A | Yes, carefully |
| Status | Rare | N/A | Luxury-only |
