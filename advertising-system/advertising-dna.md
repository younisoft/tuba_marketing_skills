## 1. Advertising Philosophy

> **Tuba does not advertise listings. Tuba advertises relief.**

Every competitor in the Saudi market (see competitor-analysis.md) advertises the same underlying commodity — inventory, search speed, licensing compliance — because that's what a real estate marketplace *is*. None of them advertise the *feeling* of the process going right. Tuba's advertising philosophy is to make every piece of creative answer one question before any other: **does this make the viewer feel less alone in a hard decision?**

This is not a soft, decoration-only philosophy — it is a disciplined creative filter:

| If a piece of creative... | Then... |
|---|---|
| Leads with a feature or spec | Rewrite it to lead with the moment/feeling that feature resolves (see copywriting.md's Hook formulas) |
| Could run unchanged for any competitor | It has failed the philosophy — it isn't distinctly Tuba |
| Makes a claim without a visible way to verify it | Add the FAL/license trust element (see design-components.md) — warmth without verifiability is just marketing |
| Uses fear, urgency, or pressure tactics | Reject it — see §5 Forbidden Territory below |

## 2. Brand Promise

> **"You will never have to figure this out alone."**

Reconstructed directly from the logo brief's own language (project-analysis.md §2: "relief," "guidance," "helping people find their place in the world") and sharpened against the competitive white space identified in competitor-analysis.md §11 (no competitor has claimed emotional/guidance territory). This single sentence is the test every headline, campaign concept, and product-marketing decision should be checked against.

**Supporting proof points (why the promise is credible, not just nice-sounding):**
- FAL/REGA-licensed listings, visibly and elegantly badged (see design-components.md)
- A request-first matching flow that removes the burden of endless browsing (Tuba's own existing, under-marketed product strength — competitor-analysis.md §3)
- Bilingual-by-design communication, not bilingual-by-translation (current-brand-analysis.md §2)
- Human support behind the technology, not a pure self-serve black box

## 3. Core Advertising Principles

1. **The customer is the hero. Tuba is the guide.** (Directly adapted from the Hook-Engage-Connect-Solve-Motivate-Decide framework in the IKEA content methodology reference — see copywriting.md §1 for full attribution and adaptation.) Never write copy where Tuba is the subject of the sentence when the customer's life could be instead.
2. **Warmth and compliance are not opposites.** Every competitor treats trust signaling as legalistic; Tuba treats it as reassurance. State the license, the verification, the process — but in the voice of someone who cares whether it goes well for you, not someone protecting themselves legally.
3. **Show the place, not just the property.** Leverage the desert/lifestyle icon language (iconography.md §4) and photography direction (visual-system.md §6) to sell "a place in the world," matching the logo's own founding concept — never a bare floor plan or a generic skyscraper stock photo.
4. **One idea per asset.** Real estate marketing is naturally cluttered (price, specs, location, CTA, license). Advertising DNA discipline means every asset still earns the right to one single takeaway — everything else is secondary information, not competing headlines.
5. **Bilingual parity, not bilingual afterthought.** An Arabic asset is never "the English ad translated" — see current-brand-analysis.md §2 for why this is structural to the brand, not a nice-to-have.
6. **Never win on volume claims.** Aqar owns the "biggest numbers" narrative (competitor-analysis.md §7); competing there is a losing frame for a challenger brand. Win on relevance and warmth per person, not scale across everyone.

## 4. Brand Emotions

### 4.1 What Tuba should make people feel

| Emotion | Why it's core | Where it shows up |
|---|---|---|
| **Relief** | Directly from the founding brand brief | Copy that names the anxiety before resolving it (copywriting.md) |
| **Belonging** | "A place in the world," not just a property | Photography direction, campaign concepts (campaign-playbook.md) |
| **Confidence** | Clarity replaces confusion | Data/market content, transparent process communication (content-system.md) |
| **Warmth** | Differentiates from every green, numbers-first, badge-first competitor | Voice, color, imagery — the whole system |
| **Quiet pride** | Especially in ownership moments, National Day, Saudi-specific content | Seasonal campaigns (campaign-playbook.md) |

### 4.2 What Tuba should never make people feel
- Rushed or pressured (no artificial urgency — "only 2 units left!" tactics contradict the brand promise)
- Talked down to (no over-explaining basic concepts to an audience that includes sophisticated investors)
- Surveilled or sold-to (no aggressive retargeting-style copy tone: "we noticed you were looking at...")
- Small next to the platform's own scale claims — never make the *brand* the impressive one; make the *customer's decision* the impressive one (Principle 1)

## 5. Forbidden Territory (explicit boundaries)

- No fear-based urgency ("act now or lose it")
- No disparaging competitors by name or implication (also a REGA advertising-bylaw requirement — competitor-analysis.md §6)
- No unverifiable superlatives ("the best," "number one") unless independently, publicly citable
- No imagery or copy that treats a home purchase as primarily a status symbol (conflicts with the "relief/belonging" emotional core — reserve status-oriented framing exclusively for explicitly luxury-segment campaigns, see campaign-playbook.md)

## 6. Brand Archetype

Using the standard Jungian brand-archetype framework as a working tool (not as jargon to appear in customer-facing copy):

**Primary archetype: The Sage (Guide)** — a brand that helps people understand, navigate, and gain clarity. Evidence: the founding brief's own words are "guidance," "relief," "helping people find their place" — this is Sage/Guide language, not Hero, Ruler, or Jester language.

**Secondary archetype: The Caregiver** — warmth, protection, putting the customer's wellbeing first. Evidence: "trust, warmth... sense of home" and the rounded, comfort-oriented shape language (current-brand-analysis.md §2).

**Explicitly not:**
- **The Ruler** (dominance, "the biggest/best") — this is Aqar's territory (numbers-first authority), and claiming it would put Tuba in a fight it doesn't need to have
- **The Hero** (the brand as the one who conquers) — Principle 1 makes the *customer* the hero; the brand playing hero would contradict the entire DNA
- **The Jester** (humor/irreverence) — mismatched to a high-stakes financial/life decision category

**Working shorthand:** *Tuba is the wise, warm friend who happens to know the Saudi real estate system inside and out — not the biggest voice in the room, the most useful one.*

## 7. Communication Pillars

1. **Clarity** — explain, don't just list (educational content, transparent process copy)
2. **Warmth** — human register in every touchpoint, including product/transactional copy (closing the gap identified in project-analysis.md §6)
3. **Proof** — every warm claim is backed by a visible, verifiable trust signal (never warmth without substance)
4. **Belonging** — Saudi place, culture, and lifestyle woven through visual and verbal content, not just "houses"

## 8. Trust Pillars

1. **Regulatory transparency** — FAL/REGA licensing displayed as a design-integrated badge (visual-system.md, design-components.md), never buried
2. **Process transparency** — state fees, steps, and timelines plainly, mirroring the "register → verify → choose → sign" clarity pattern that gives government platforms like Sakani their credibility (competitor-analysis.md §8)
3. **Verification with enforcement** — any badge or claim of "verified" must be operationally true, learning directly from Bayut's review-sentiment gap (competitor-analysis.md §4) — a broken promise here costs more brand equity than never making the claim
4. **Human backup** — technology plus a real support layer, never a pure self-serve black box

---

## 9. Consistency Rules

- Every asset, regardless of channel, must be traceable to the Brand Promise (§2) — if it isn't, it doesn't ship
- The purple/coral color hierarchy (visual-system.md §1.7) is never renegotiated per-campaign — campaigns get seasonal *content*, never a seasonal *palette swap*
- Voice adapts in register by channel (formal for LinkedIn/PR, casual for TikTok/Snapchat — see social-media-playbook.md) but never in *values* — warmth and clarity apply everywhere, even in a 6-second Snap ad
- Arabic is never a downstream translation step — every campaign is conceived bilingually from brief stage (see copywriting.md §8)

## 10. Advertising Personality

| Dimension | Tuba's position | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **Luxury level** | **Accessible-premium**, not exclusive-luxury by default | The brand promise is relief for everyone making a major life decision, not status signaling for a narrow elite — luxury register is reserved for explicit luxury-segment campaigns (campaign-playbook.md), never the default tone |
| **Professional level** | **High** — this is a licensed, regulated, high-stakes category | Never sacrifice credibility for casualness; professionalism and warmth are compatible (Principle 2) |
| **Approachability** | **High** | Directly from the founding brief's "warmth, friendliness, approachability" language for the rounded logo shapes |
| **Confidence** | **Quiet confidence, not loud confidence** | State things plainly and back them with proof rather than superlative claims (§5) — confidence shows in clarity, not volume |
| **Authority** | **Earned through transparency and data, not claimed through scale numbers** | Differentiates from Aqar's numbers-first authority model (competitor-analysis.md §7) |
| **Innovation** | **Practical innovation, quietly delivered** | The founding brief references virtual tours, data, and technology — but always in service of reducing friction, never as a showy "look how advanced we are" flex |
| **Modernity** | **Modern but warm** — never cold-tech, never glassy/corporate | Matches the rounded, soft shape language throughout the visual system |

## 11. Brand Voice (summary — full system in copywriting.md)

**Tuba sounds like:** a knowledgeable friend who happens to work in real estate, explaining something clearly because they want you to get it right — not a company reciting features, and not a salesperson closing a deal.

**Five voice adjectives (the working test for any copy):**
1. **Warm** — never cold or corporate
2. **Clear** — never jargon-heavy or vague
3. **Grounded** — never hyped or superlative
4. **Guiding** — never passive; always moves the reader one step forward
5. **Respectful** — never condescending, never pressuring

Full vocabulary, sentence-length rules, and forbidden phrases are specified in [copywriting.md](copywriting.md).

## 12. Desired Customer Feeling — By Journey Stage

| Stage | Feeling to create | How |
|---|---|---|
| **First exposure** (ad/social) | "This one seems different — warmer" | Distinct purple/coral visual system, human-first headline (not feature-first) |
| **Consideration** (browsing/content) | "This actually makes sense to me now" | Clarity-first educational content (content-system.md) |
| **Decision** (listing/agent contact) | "I'm not doing this alone" | Visible trust signals, human support prompts, request-first matching highlighted |
| **Post-transaction** (retention/referral) | "I want to tell someone about this" | Warm, low-pressure retention content — never a hard-sell re-engagement tone |
