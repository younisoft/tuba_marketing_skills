# Trust Behavior

> **Part of:** [`../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md`](../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md)
> **The reframe this document insists on:** trust is not a badge. `advertising-dna.md §8` already establishes the Trust Pillars as claims (regulatory transparency, process transparency, verification, human backup) — this document specifies how each becomes something the product *does*, continuously, whether or not anyone is looking for the badge that represents it.

---

## Transparency, as Behavior

Every fee, condition, and step is visible before it's needed, not disclosed only when a user asks or reaches a paywall moment. A transparent product shows the total cost of a transaction before the final confirmation screen, not as a surprise at the last step — this is the behavioral test that separates genuine transparency from technically-legal disclosure buried in terms.

## Honesty, as Behavior

The product tells a user something it would rather not have to say — a listing that's about to be delisted, a market where prices are moving against their stated budget, a mismatch between their expectations and what's realistically available — proactively, before being asked, even when the honest answer risks losing the interaction. `narrative-system/HUMAN_TRUTHS.md` §33–34 (*"trust isn't given to whoever asks for it first. It's given to whoever explains without being asked"*) is the literal product requirement here, not just a conversational ideal.

## Expectation Management, as Behavior

Every promise the product makes about timing, process, or outcome is calibrated to what's actually achievable, not to what sounds most reassuring in the moment. If a valuation typically takes 48 hours, the product says 48 hours — never a more optimistic number chosen because it reads better, then quietly missed.

## Respect, as Behavior

The product never asks for more information, time, or attention than a given step genuinely requires. A form with fields that exist for internal convenience rather than the user's actual need is a respect failure, regardless of how politely those fields are labeled.

## Privacy, as Behavior

Data gathered for personalization is used to serve the person it was gathered from, never surfaced in a way that makes them feel tracked (`PRODUCT_PHILOSOPHY.md`'s "never narrate the use back as surveillance" rule) and never shared with a third party (including another Tuba user, a broker, or a developer) beyond what the person explicitly understood and agreed to when they gave it.

## Time, as Behavior

A stated wait is honored; if it can't be, the person is told before the deadline passes, not after. `EXPERIENCE_PRINCIPLES.md` §3's waiting-state design and this trust pillar are the same commitment viewed from two angles — one is UX, one is integrity, and a product that gets the first right without the second has only solved half the problem.

## Fairness, as Behavior

Pricing, ranking, and access are consistent for comparable users — the platform doesn't show a materially different price or a materially worse position in search results based on factors the user has no visibility into or control over. Where a difference is legitimate (a licensed broker's paid placement, clearly labeled), it's disclosed; where it isn't legitimate, it doesn't happen.

## Verification, Enforced

`advertising-dna.md §8.3`'s standard — *"any badge or claim of 'verified' must be operationally true"* — means every "verified" or "licensed" label on the platform is backed by an active check, re-validated on a real cadence, not a one-time badge that can go stale while still displaying. A false or lapsed verification badge is treated as a P0 defect, not a content-team fix.

## The One Question Every Trust Decision Answers

**If this exact behavior were fully visible to the user — the internal logic, not just the output — would it still look like something Tuba would be proud of?** A ranking algorithm, a pricing rule, or a data use that only survives because it's hidden has already failed the standard, regardless of whether it's ever actually exposed.
