# Service Philosophy

> **Part of:** [`../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md`](../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md)
> **Scope:** every human-delivered touchpoint — call center, WhatsApp support, in-branch conversations, broker-facing account management, complaint handling. Where [`PRODUCT_PHILOSOPHY.md`](PRODUCT_PHILOSOPHY.md) governs what software does, this document governs what *people employed by or representing Tuba* do, on the phone or in person, when the brand is being performed live by a human being with no undo button.

---

## The Governing Idea

`advertising-dna.md`'s Sage/Caregiver archetype (§6) is easy to write into an ad. It is much harder to sustain across a real phone call with a frustrated customer at the end of a long day. Service philosophy exists to close that gap: **every person who represents Tuba on a call, a chat, or in person is trained to be the same wise, warm friend the advertising describes — not a script-reader defending the company, and not a salesperson closing a deal.**

## How Tuba Answers the Phone

Within the first sentence, by name, and with the caller's context already visible — never "please hold while I pull up your file" for someone who has already explained themselves in the app. If the context genuinely isn't available, the agent says so plainly and asks once, briefly, rather than making the caller feel like they're starting over.

## How Emails Are Written by Support Staff

Every principle in [`EMAIL_BEHAVIOR.md`](EMAIL_BEHAVIOR.md) applies to human-written support email exactly as it applies to automated email — a customer cannot tell, and shouldn't need to, whether a reply was templated or typed live, because both are held to the same clarity and warmth standard.

## How WhatsApp Messages Are Handled

WhatsApp is where Saudi customers expect the fastest, least formal, most human register Tuba uses anywhere (`brand-intelligence/market-psychology.md`'s channel findings on messaging-app expectations). Replies are short, specific, and sent by a person who signs their first name — never a faceless "Tuba Support" identity issuing paragraph-length policy statements in a channel built for a quick, human back-and-forth.

## How Complaints Are Managed

A complaint is treated as the highest-priority signal a customer can send, not an inconvenience to be resolved efficiently and closed. The sequence is fixed: acknowledge the specific issue (never "we understand your frustration" as a generic opener), take ownership without deflecting to "the system" or "policy," give a real timeline for resolution, and follow up on that timeline without being asked. This inherits directly from `narrative-system/CONVERSATION_SYSTEM.md`'s "How Tuba Responds to Criticism": *"You're right, and here's what we're doing about it,"* not defensiveness dressed as professionalism.

## How Employees Apologize

Specifically, once, and without over-explaining. A good Tuba apology names the actual failure ("we told you the valuation would be ready Tuesday and it wasn't"), states what's being done about it, and stops — it does not spiral into a longer justification that shifts the emotional labor of the conversation back onto the customer.

## How Employees Celebrate Customers

Quietly and specifically, mirroring `narrative-system/CONVERSATION_SYSTEM.md`'s "How Tuba Celebrates" — an agent who closes a call by naming the actual thing worth celebrating ("this is your first home, that's a big deal") does more brand work than any scripted congratulations line ever could, precisely because it wasn't scripted.

## How Tuba Rejects Requests

Directly, with a real reason, and — wherever possible — with an alternative. "That's not something we can do because X, but here's what I can do instead" preserves the relationship even when the literal answer is no. A rejection with no reason, or a reason that's obviously a deflection ("that's just our policy"), is treated as a service failure even if the underlying decision was correct.

## How Tuba Explains Delays

Before being asked, with a real cause stated plainly, and with a next update time the customer can actually hold the team to. `narrative-system/HUMAN_TRUTHS.md` §36 — *"people forgive a slow answer, they rarely forgive a confident wrong one"* — is the operating principle: a delay explained honestly costs less trust than a deadline confidently promised and then missed.

## Internal Escalation

Any service interaction that a frontline agent cannot resolve within the standard has one clear, fast escalation path — never a case that quietly stalls because no one owns it. `HUMAN_ASSISTANCE.md` specifies the technical routing; this document sets the cultural expectation: an escalated case is a shared responsibility the moment it's escalated, not a handoff that ends the first agent's accountability.
