# Success Moments

> **Part of:** [`../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md`](../BRAND_BEHAVIOR_SYSTEM.md)
> **The question this document answers:** how should Tuba celebrate — saving a property, receiving keys, investment success, loan approval, a family milestone — and how much celebration is earned by each. Not every success is the same size, and treating them as if they were is the fastest way to make the biggest ones feel small.

---

## The Governing Rule: Celebration Scales With What Was Actually Risked

A five-tier scale, used consistently across product and communications:

| Tier | Example | Behavior |
|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Micro** | Saving a property, completing a search filter | A small, quiet visual acknowledgment (a subtle animation, a color change) — no modal, no message, nothing that interrupts |
| **Tier 2 — Progress** | Completing a profile, submitting a full application | A brief, specific confirmation naming what was completed and what happens next — no fanfare, just clear forward motion |
| **Tier 3 — Milestone** | Loan pre-approval, a scheduled viewing confirmed | A dedicated, warm moment — still restrained, but acknowledged as genuinely good news, with a clear next step attached |
| **Tier 4 — Major** | Loan final approval, an accepted offer | A real celebratory moment — specific to the person's actual situation, never a templated "Congratulations!" graphic reused across every user |
| **Tier 5 — Life** | Receiving keys, closing on an investment property | The most restrained tone paired with the deepest specificity — this is `narrative-system/CONVERSATION_SYSTEM.md`'s "How Tuba Celebrates" in its fullest form: naming the actual thing worth celebrating, once, then stepping back so the person gets to feel it without an audience |

## How Much Restraint

The counterintuitive rule, stated plainly: **the bigger the moment, the more restraint the brand shows.** A Tier 5 moment (getting keys to a first home) does not need bright confetti animation and exclamation points — it needs the platform to say something true and specific, briefly, and then get out of the way. Big emotions don't need amplification; they need room. Small emotions (Tier 1–2) can afford a little more visual delight precisely because they're not carrying much emotional weight to begin with — a saved-listing animation can be playful in a way a keys-handover message never should be.

## The Customer's Decision Is the Achievement

Every success moment, regardless of tier, is framed around what the *person* did, not what Tuba did for them — direct continuation of `narrative-system/CONVERSATION_SYSTEM.md`'s "How Tuba Congratulates": *"You did the hard part — you decided. We're glad we got to help,"* never *"Tuba made this happen for you."* This is checked in every success-moment copy review the same way a headline is checked against `advertising-dna.md` Principle 1.

## Success Moments Are Specific, Not Templated

A Tier 4 or 5 message names the real thing — the neighborhood, the number of months searched, the fact that this is a first home versus a fifth investment property — because a generic congratulations at the platform's biggest emotional moments is a worse failure than a generic one at a small moment. `EXPERIENCE_PRINCIPLES.md` §7's specificity standard applies with the most force exactly here.

## Success Moments Respect the Household

Per `EXPERIENCE_PRINCIPLES.md` §10, a major milestone (keys, closing) often belongs to a family, not one account holder — the product allows a moment to be shared (a shareable, low-key confirmation, not a loud social broadcast by default) rather than assuming the celebration is a solitary, individual experience.

## What Success Moments Must Never Do

- Never celebrate on Tuba's behalf ("We're so proud of ourselves for helping you!") — the celebration is always about the person.
- Never reuse the exact same asset, animation, or copy across every user at the same tier — even a Tier 3 milestone deserves at least one specific detail.
- Never turn a genuine milestone into an unprompted upsell moment (e.g., pitching a second property immediately after a first-home celebration) — the moment is allowed to just be what it is.
