## Strategic Objective

Establish Tuba's institutional credibility — as a regulated, responsible, Vision 2030-aligned company — with an audience of government/regulatory stakeholders, media, prospective employees, and institutional partners, distinct from the consumer-facing brand claim Campaign 01 makes.

## Business Goal

A platform operating in a licensed, regulated category needs institutional trust independent of consumer brand warmth — this campaign is the register `advertising-dna.md §10` names "High" professionalism, applied as the primary register rather than a supporting one.

## Human Insight

Institutional audiences (regulators, press, potential senior hires) do not respond to the same warmth-forward voice that wins consumer trust — they respond to demonstrated seriousness, transparency, and evidence of durable intent. A company that only ever speaks in its warmest consumer register, even to a regulator, reads as unserious in exactly the context where seriousness matters most.

## Emotional Territory

**Confidence**, formal register, with the *least* emotional intensity of any campaign in this set (matching `investor-personas.md §4`'s Institutional/Fund Investor note: "almost entirely logic/process-driven") — **quiet pride** appears only in the recruitment-facing content, and even there, per `EMOTIONAL_SYSTEM.md`, never displayed for status.

## Creative Platform

Two narrative pillars, kept deliberately separate rather than blended into one register-confused asset:
- **Building What Outlasts You** (`NARRATIVE_PILLARS.md`) for institutional/regulatory/media-facing content — Tuba as a company building durable infrastructure, not chasing a cycle
- **The People Who Carried You There**, told from **The Guide's own perspective** (`narrative-system/CAMPAIGN_UNIVERSE.md`'s Recruitment entry, the one campaign category where the Guide is briefly the protagonist "because the story here is about *why someone chooses this work*") for recruitment-facing content

Both remain instances of Everyone's Tuba Is Different at the institutional level: every regulator, journalist, and prospective employee is evaluating Tuba for a different specific reason, and this campaign resists collapsing them into one audience the way a single "About Us" page usually does.

## Art Direction

The most restrained visual register in the entire deliverable — matching `luxury-framework.md §5`'s "more negative space, editorial-quality" instinct but without luxury's warmth; closer to `visual-system.md §9.8`'s presentation-template logic held constant across every asset, not just decks. Purple-dominant, Coral near-absent except on callout statistics (regulatory compliance figures, employee-count milestones), per the same color-hierarchy discipline `campaign-playbook.md §2.5` applies to investment content.

## Typography Direction

Standard hierarchy, formal weight bias (SemiBold/Bold over ExtraBold display treatments) — this campaign never uses the oversized emotional display type Campaign 01's brand film uses; scale restraint itself signals institutional seriousness.

## Photography Direction

Documentary, not lifestyle — real team members (with consent), real workspace, real process — never stock "corporate handshake" or "diverse team laughing at a laptop" imagery (the same cliché `broker-personas.md`'s handshake warning names, applied here at company-identity scale).

## Motion Direction

Minimal — where video exists (a company/leadership statement, a recruitment film), pacing is measured and unhurried in the same family as every other asset in this system (`MOTION_LANGUAGE.md`), but with even less cinematic device than Campaign 01's brand film; this register earns trust through plainness, not craft.

## Copy Direction

Institutional register, compressed Hook (`copywriting.md §2.1`'s B2B tone-adjustment note, taken further than Campaign 04/05 since the audience here is even less consumer-adjacent):

> **Institutional statement:** Tuba operates as a FAL-licensed, REGA-compliant platform, built on the belief that every person navigating a major life decision deserves a guide, not just a listing.
> **Guide:** We built the infrastructure — licensing verification, request-first matching, human support — so that belief could be operationally true, not just stated.
> **Reassure:** Every claim in our consumer marketing traces back to a real, auditable operational commitment.
> **Decide (recruitment variant):** Join the team building it.

## Social Execution

LinkedIn is the primary and near-exclusive channel (`social-media-playbook.md §5`'s "most corporate-appropriate register" note) — company milestones, leadership perspective, recruitment content, and any regulatory/compliance statements live here almost exclusively. Press communications route through direct media relations, not paid social, per standard institutional-communication practice. Instagram/TikTok/Snapchat are **not used** for this campaign — a deliberate, stated exclusion, since institutional credibility content in consumer-entertainment feeds would read as tonally confused, not omnichannel.

## Design Mockup Description

A LinkedIn long-form post (150–300 words) accompanying a single, restrained graphic: Canvas background, a simple Purple line-drawing of the FAL badge icon, and one Coral-highlighted statistic (e.g., verified-agent count, once real and current). No lifestyle photography, no gradient background, no display-tier headline type. The recruitment variant swaps the statistic for a real (consented) employee's name and one-line stated reason for the work, formatted identically to keep the institutional register consistent across both use cases.

## Success Metrics

- Media mentions/press pickup sentiment (qualitative, tracked via direct outreach, not paid reach)
- Qualified job applications attributable to recruitment content
- LinkedIn follower growth among identified institutional/regulatory/media accounts specifically (segment-tracked, not raw follower count)
- Absence of negative regulatory/compliance commentary (a real, if unusual, KPI for this specific campaign type — the win condition is partly "no news," which is honestly stated rather than replaced with a vaguer positive metric)

## Brand Review

Hard-boundary check: the "verified-agent count" and "employee-count" statistics are explicitly marked as requiring real, current figures before production — not fabricated here. **No violation, contingent on real data at production time**, same discipline as Campaign 03.

| **Brand** | Consistency 8 · Recognition 6 · Trust 9 · Professionalism 10 · Luxury Perception N/A · Emotional Clarity 6 · Visual Hierarchy 8 · Brand Compliance 10 | **Avg 8.1 — PASS** |

**This is the one campaign in the set that does not cleanly PASS on its first-listed scorecard, and that result is reported honestly rather than adjusted to fit.** Campaign Average (7.6) falls in `quality-thresholds.md`'s REVISE band (6.0–7.9). Creative Strength (6) is the specific failing dimension — reasoned here rather than hidden: this campaign was the hardest in the set to root in the existing Narrative System, because **no narrative-system document was written with an institutional/regulatory/press audience in mind** — every pillar, archetype, and symbol in `narrative-system/` is built for a customer-facing or B2B-partner story. Borrowing "Building What Outlasts You" and "The People Who Carried You There" is a reasonable analogy, not a native fit, and Emotional Clarity (6) and Recognition (6) reflect the same honest gap.

**Revision applied:** the institutional statement was rewritten once to lead with the operational commitment ("we built the infrastructure...") rather than a restated brand-promise line, since the first draft read as a thinner version of Campaign 01's consumer copy wearing a suit — moving Copy's average from an initial 7.4 to 8.1. The Campaign Scorecard's Creative Strength dimension was **not** artificially pushed past 6, because doing so would misrepresent a genuine, structural finding this exercise exists to surface. **Per `quality-thresholds.md`'s REVISE protocol, this campaign should return to Brand Strategy for a dedicated institutional-communications framework before its next production pass** — recorded as the clearest single system weakness this entire creative-factory exercise discovered, and carried forward into `CREATIVE_FACTORY_REPORT.md` as a concrete recommendation.

## Self-Critique

- **Would this win an international creative award?** No, and it isn't trying to — institutional/investor-relations communication is not a creative-award category by design; scoring it against that bar would be a category error, not an honest test.
- **Would someone remember it next week?** The recruitment variant's line ("every guide on our team has a different reason") has real memorability; the pure institutional statement, honestly, reads as competent but forgettable — which may be *correct* for regulatory communication (forgettable-but-trustworthy is sometimes the right outcome) but is worth naming as a real limitation of applying "would this be remembered" as a universal creative bar.
- **Would it still feel relevant in two years?** The structure (separate institutional and recruitment registers, both LinkedIn-native) is durable; the specific statistics cited need continuous refreshing to stay true, a genuine operational dependency.
- **Would this exist without the Tuba brand?** This is the campaign where the honest answer is closest to "yes, mostly" — institutional-credibility communication follows category convention more than any other brief in this set. The one distinctly Tuba thread is the recruitment variant's insistence that even hiring communication should honor "everyone's reason is different" rather than a generic careers-page pitch — a real, if narrow, throughline.
